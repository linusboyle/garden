***Nixon in China*** is an [opera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opera "Opera") in three acts by [John Adams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Adams_(composer) "John Adams (composer)") with a [libretto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libretto "Libretto") by [Alice Goodman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alice_Goodman "Alice Goodman"). Adams's first opera, it was inspired by U.S. president [Richard Nixon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Nixon "Richard Nixon")'s [1972 visit to the People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1972_visit_by_Richard_Nixon_to_China "1972 visit by Richard Nixon to China"). The work premiered at the [Houston Grand Opera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Houston_Grand_Opera "Houston Grand Opera") on October 22, 1987, in a production by [Peter Sellars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Sellars "Peter Sellars") with choreography by [Mark Morris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Morris_(choreographer) "Mark Morris (choreographer)"). When Sellars approached Adams with the idea for the opera in 1983, Adams was initially reluctant, but eventually decided that the work could be a study in how myths come to be, and accepted the project. Goodman's libretto was the result of considerable research into Nixon's visit, though she disregarded most sources published after the 1972 trip.

To create the sounds he sought, Adams augmented the orchestra with a large [saxophone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saxophone "Saxophone") section, additional percussion, and electronic [synthesizer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthesizer "Synthesizer"). Although sometimes described as [minimalist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimalism_(music) "Minimalism (music)"), the score displays a variety of musical styles, embracing minimalism after the manner of [Philip Glass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_Glass "Philip Glass") alongside passages echoing 19th-century composers such as [Wagner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Wagner "Richard Wagner") and [Johann Strauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Strauss_II "Johann Strauss II"). With these ingredients, Adams mixes [Stravinskian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igor_Stravinsky "Igor Stravinsky") 20th-century [neoclassicism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoclassicism_(music) "Neoclassicism (music)"), jazz references, and [big band](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_band "Big band") sounds reminiscent of Nixon's youth in the 1930s. The combination of these elements varies frequently, to reflect changes in the onstage action.

Following the 1987 premiere, the opera received mixed reviews; some critics dismissed the work, predicting it would soon vanish. However, it has been presented on many occasions since, in both Europe and North America, and has been recorded at least five times. In 2011, the opera received its [Metropolitan Opera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_Opera "Metropolitan Opera") debut, a production based on the original sets, and in the same year was given an abstract production in Toronto by the [Canadian Opera Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_Opera_Company "Canadian Opera Company"). Recent critical opinion has tended to recognize the work as a significant and lasting contribution to American opera.

## Background\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nixon_in_China&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Background")\]

### Historical background\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nixon_in_China&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Historical background")\]

[![](220px-President_Richard_Nixon_and_Mao_Zedong.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:President_Richard_Nixon_and_Mao_Zedong.jpg)

[Richard Nixon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Nixon "Richard Nixon") (right) meets [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong"), February 1972

During his rise to power, [Richard Nixon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Nixon "Richard Nixon") became known as a [leading anti-communist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_Un-American_Activities_Committee "House Un-American Activities Committee"). After he became president in 1969, Nixon saw advantages in improving relations with China and the Soviet Union; he hoped that [détente](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%C3%A9tente "Détente") would put pressure on the [North Vietnamese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Vietnam "North Vietnam") to end the [Vietnam War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam_War "Vietnam War"), and he might be able to manipulate the two main communist powers to the benefit of the United States.[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-miller-1)

Nixon laid the groundwork for his overture to China even before he became president, writing in *[Foreign Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_Affairs "Foreign Affairs")* a year before his election: "There is no place on this small planet for a billion of its potentially most able people to live in angry isolation."[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-miller-1) Assisting him in this venture was his [National Security Advisor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Security_Advisor_(United_States) "National Security Advisor (United States)"), [Henry Kissinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Kissinger "Henry Kissinger"), with whom the President worked closely, bypassing Cabinet officials. With relations between the Soviet Union and China at a nadir—[border clashes between the two](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino%E2%80%93Soviet_border_conflict "Sino–Soviet border conflict") took place during Nixon's first year in office—Nixon sent private word to the Chinese that he desired closer relations. A breakthrough came in early 1971, when [Chinese Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") chairman [Mao Zedong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mao_Zedong "Mao Zedong") invited a team of American [table tennis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Table_tennis "Table tennis") players to visit China and play against top Chinese players. Nixon followed up by sending Kissinger to China for clandestine meetings with Chinese officials.[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-miller-1)

The announcement that Nixon would visit China in 1972 made world headlines. Almost immediately, the Soviet Union also invited Nixon for a visit, and improved US-Soviet relations led to the [Strategic Arms Limitation Talks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategic_Arms_Limitation_Talks "Strategic Arms Limitation Talks") (SALT). Nixon's [visit to China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1972_Nixon_visit_to_China "1972 Nixon visit to China") was followed closely by many Americans, and the scenes of him there were widely aired on television.[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-miller-1) Chinese premier [Zhou Enlai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhou_Enlai "Zhou Enlai") stated that the handshake he and Nixon had shared on the airport tarmac at the beginning of the visit was "over the vastest distance in the world, 25 years of no communication".[\[2\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKabaservice,_Ch._11_(unpaginated)-2) Nixon's change, from virulent anti-communist to the American leader who took the first step in improving relations with a [great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_power "Great power") communist power, led to a new political adage, "[Only Nixon could go to China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_goes_to_China "Nixon goes to China")."[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-miller-1)

### Inception\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nixon_in_China&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Inception")\]

[![](220px-Nixons_exit_AFO_in_China_1972.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Nixons_exit_AFO_in_China_1972.jpg)

[Richard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Nixon "Richard Nixon") and [Pat Nixon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pat_Nixon "Pat Nixon") descend the steps outside the *[Spirit of '76](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spirit_of_%2776_(airplane) "Spirit of '76 (airplane)")*, February 21, 1972.

In 1983, theater and opera director [Peter Sellars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Sellars "Peter Sellars") proposed to American composer [John Adams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Adams_(composer) "John Adams (composer)") that he write an opera about Nixon's 1972 visit to China.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-myth-3) Sellars was intrigued by Nixon's decision to make the visit, seeing it as both "a ridiculously cynical election ploy ... and a historical breakthrough".[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Braun_2011-02-4) Adams, who had not previously attempted an opera, was initially skeptical, assuming that Sellars was proposing a satire.[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Park_making-5) Sellars persisted, however, and Adams, who had interested himself in the origin of myths, came to believe the opera could show how mythic origins may be found in contemporary history.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-myth-3) Both men agreed that the opera would be heroic in nature, rather than poking fun at Nixon or Mao.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJohnson3-6) Sellars invited [Alice Goodman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alice_Goodman "Alice Goodman") to join the project as [librettist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libretto "Libretto"),[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Park_making-5) and the three met at the [Kennedy Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kennedy_Center "Kennedy Center") in Washington D.C. in 1985 to begin intensive study of the six characters, three American and three Chinese, upon whom the opera would focus. The trio endeavored to go beyond the stereotypes about figures such as Nixon and Chinese Communist Party Chairman Mao and to examine their personalities.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-myth-3)

[![](220px-JA-portrait-1-LW.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:JA-portrait-1-LW.jpg)

John Adams, composer, 2008

As Adams worked on the opera, he came to see Nixon, whom he had once intensely disliked, as an "interesting character", a complicated individual who sometimes showed emotion in public.[\[7\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-artsbeat-7) Adams wanted Mao to be "the Mao of the huge posters and [Great Leap Forward](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Leap_Forward "Great Leap Forward"); I cast him as a [heldentenor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heldentenor "Heldentenor")".[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-myth-3) Mao's wife, on the other hand, was to be "not just a shrieking [coloratura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coloratura "Coloratura"), but also someone who in the opera's final act can reveal her private fantasies, her erotic desires, and even a certain tragic awareness. Nixon himself is a sort of [Simon Boccanegra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simon_Boccanegra "Simon Boccanegra"), a self-doubting, lyrical, at times self-pitying melancholy baritone."[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-myth-3)

Goodman explained her characterizations:

> A writer tends to find her characters in her self, so I can tell you ... that Nixon, Pat, Mme. Mao, Kissinger and the chorus were all 'me.' And the inner lives of Mao and Chou En-Lai, who I couldn't find in myself at all, were drawn from a couple of close acquaintances.[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-matt-8)

Sellars, who was engaged at the time in staging the three [Mozart-Da Ponte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Da_Ponte_operas "Da Ponte operas") operas, became interested in the [ensembles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musical_ensemble "Musical ensemble") in those works; this interest is reflected in *Nixon in China'*s final act.[\[9\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-May_2011-02-09-9) The director encouraged Adams and Goodman to make other allusions to classical operatic forms; thus the expectant chorus that begins the work, the heroic [aria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aria "Aria") for Nixon following his entrance, and the dueling toasts in the final scene of Act 1.[\[9\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-May_2011-02-09-9) In rehearsal, Sellars revised the staging for the final scene, changing it from a banquet hall in the aftermath of a slightly alcohol-fueled dinner to the characters' bedrooms.[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Henken_1990-09-02-10)

The work required sacrifices: Goodman later noted that choruses which she loved were dropped for the improvement of the opera as a whole. The work provoked bitter arguments among the three. Nevertheless, musicologist Timothy Johnson, in his 2011 book about *Nixon in China*, noted "the result of the collaboration betrays none of these disagreements among its creators who successfully blended their differing points of view into a very satisfyingly cohesive whole".[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJohnson3-6)

## Synopsis\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nixon_in_China&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Synopsis")\]

Time: February 1972.

Place: In and around Peking.

### Act 1\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nixon_in_China&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Act 1")\]

[![](220px-Pat_Nixon_in_China.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pat_Nixon_in_China.jpg)

[Pat Nixon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pat_Nixon "Pat Nixon") is conducted on a tour of Peking, February 23, 1972.

At Peking Airport, contingents of the Chinese military await the arrival of the American presidential aircraft "[Spirit of '76](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/VC-137C_SAM_26000 "VC-137C SAM 26000")", carrying Nixon and his party. The military chorus sings the [Three Rules of Discipline and Eight Points for Attention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_Rules_of_Discipline_and_Eight_Points_for_Attention "Three Rules of Discipline and Eight Points for Attention"). After the aircraft touches down, Nixon emerges with Pat Nixon and Henry Kissinger. The president exchanges stilted greetings with the Chinese premier, Chou En-lai, who heads the welcoming party. Nixon speaks of the historical significance of the visit, and of his hopes and fears for the encounter ("News has a kind of mystery"). The scene changes to Chairman Mao's study, where the chairman awaits the arrival of the presidential party. Nixon and Kissinger enter with Chou, and Mao and the president converse in banalities as photographers record the scene. In the discussion that follows, the westerners are confused by Mao's gnomic and frequently impenetrable comments, which are amplified by his secretaries and often by Chou. The scene changes again, to the evening's banquet in the Great Hall of the People. Chou toasts the American visitors ("We have begun to celebrate the different ways") and Nixon responds ("I have attended many feasts"), after which the toasts continue as the atmosphere becomes increasingly convivial. Nixon, a politician who rose to prominence on anti-communism, announces: "Everyone, listen; just let me say one thing. I opposed China, I was wrong".

### Act 2\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nixon_in_China&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Act 2")\]

[![](220px-Ballet_during_Nixon_visit.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ballet_during_Nixon_visit.gif)

Scene from the ballet *[The Red Detachment of Women](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Detachment_of_Women_(ballet) "Red Detachment of Women (ballet)")*, witnessed by the Nixons on February 22, 1972

Pat Nixon is touring the city, with guides. Factory workers present her with a small model elephant which, she delightedly informs them, is the symbol of the [Republican Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States) "Republican Party (United States)") which her husband leads. She visits a commune where she is greeted enthusiastically, and is captivated by the children's games that she observes in the school. "I used to be a teacher many years ago", she sings, "and now I'm here to learn from you". She moves on to the [Summer Palace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summer_Palace "Summer Palace"), where in a contemplative aria ("This is prophetic") she envisages a peaceful future for the world. In the evening the presidential party, as guests of Mao's wife Chiang Ch'ing, attends the [Peking Opera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peking_Opera "Peking Opera") for a performance of a political ballet-opera *[The Red Detachment of Women](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Detachment_of_Women_(ballet) "Red Detachment of Women (ballet)")*. This depicts the downfall of a cruel and unscrupulous landlord's agent (played by an actor who strongly resembles Kissinger) at the hands of brave women revolutionary workers. The action deeply affects the Nixons; at one point Pat rushes onstage to help a peasant girl she thinks is being whipped to death. As the stage action ends, Chiang Ch'ing, angry at the apparent misinterpretation of the piece's message, sings a harsh aria ("I am the wife of Mao Tse-tung"), praising the [Cultural Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_Revolution "Cultural Revolution") and glorifying her own part in it. A revolutionary chorus echoes her words.

### Act 3\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nixon_in_China&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Act 3")\]

On the last evening of the visit, as they lie in their respective beds, the chief protagonists muse on their personal histories in a surreal series of interwoven dialogues. Nixon and Pat recall the struggles of their youth; Nixon evokes wartime memories ("Sitting round the radio"). Mao and Chiang Ch'ing dance together, as the Chairman remembers "the tasty little starlet" who came to his headquarters in the early days of the revolution. As they reminisce, Chiang Ch'ing asserts that "the revolution must not end". Chou meditates alone; the opera finishes on a thoughtful note with his aria "I am old and I cannot sleep", asking: "How much of what we did was good?" The early morning birdcalls are summoning him to resume his work, while "outside this room the chill of grace lies heavy on the morning grass".

## Performance history\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nixon_in_China&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Performance history")\]

The work was a joint commission from the [Houston Grand Opera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Houston_Grand_Opera "Houston Grand Opera"), the [Brooklyn Academy of Music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brooklyn_Academy_of_Music "Brooklyn Academy of Music"), [Netherlands Opera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netherlands_Opera "Netherlands Opera") and the [Washington Opera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washington_Opera "Washington Opera"),[\[14\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Holden_1987-07-28-14) all of which planned to mount early productions of the opera.[\[12\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Rhein_1987-10-30-12) Fearful that the work might be challenged as defamatory or not in the public domain, Houston Grand Opera obtained insurance to cover such an eventuality.[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Henken_1990-09-02-10) Before its stage premiere, the opera was presented in concert form in May 1987 in San Francisco, with intermission discussions led by Adams. According to the *[Los Angeles Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times "Los Angeles Times")* review, a number of audience members left as the work proceeded.[\[15\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Bernheimer_1987-05-25-15)

*Nixon in China* formally premiered on the Brown Stage at the new [Wortham Theater Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wortham_Theater_Center "Wortham Theater Center") in Houston on October 22, 1987, with [John DeMain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_DeMain "John DeMain") conducting the [Houston Grand Opera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Houston_Grand_Opera "Houston Grand Opera").[\[13\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Holmes_1987-10-18-13) Former president Nixon was invited, and was sent a copy of the libretto; however, his staff indicated that he was unable to attend, due to illness and an impending publication deadline.[\[16\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-nixonnixon-16) A Nixon representative later stated that the former president disliked seeing himself on television or other media, and had little interest in opera.[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Henken_1990-09-02-10) According to Adams, he was later told by former Nixon lawyer [Leonard Garment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonard_Garment "Leonard Garment") that Nixon was highly interested in everything written about him, and so likely saw the Houston production when it was televised on [PBS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PBS "PBS")'s *[Great Performances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Performances "Great Performances")*.[\[17\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJohnson4-17)

The piece opened in conjunction with the annual meeting of the Music Critics Association, guaranteeing what the *[Houston Chronicle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Houston_Chronicle "Houston Chronicle")* described as a "very discriminating audience".[\[18\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-critic-18) Members of the association also attended meetings with the opera's production team.[\[18\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-critic-18) When [Carolann Page](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolann_Page "Carolann Page"), originating Pat Nixon, waved to the audience in character as [First Lady](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Lady "First Lady"), many waved back at her.[\[19\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Ewing_1987-10-24-19) Adams responded to complaints that the words were difficult to understand (no [supertitles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surtitles "Surtitles") were provided) by indicating that it is not necessary that all the words be understood on first seeing an opera.[\[16\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-nixonnixon-16) The audience's general reaction was expressed by what the *[Los Angeles Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times "Los Angeles Times")* termed "polite applause", the descent of the *Spirit of '76* being the occasion for clapping from both the onstage chorus and from the viewers in the opera house.[\[20\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Bernheimer_1987-10-24-20)

When the opera reached the [Brooklyn Academy of Music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brooklyn_Academy_of_Music "Brooklyn Academy of Music"), six weeks after the world premiere, there was again applause during the *Spirit of '76*'s descent. Chou En-lai's toast, addressed by baritone [Sanford Sylvan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanford_Sylvan "Sanford Sylvan") directly to the audience, brought what pianist and writer William R. Braun called "a shocked hush of chastened admiration".[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Braun_2011-02-4) The meditative Act 3 also brought silence, followed at its conclusion by a storm of applause.[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Braun_2011-02-4) On March 26, 1988, the work opened at the John F. Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts in Washington, DC, where Nixon's emergence from the plane was again met with applause.[\[21\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Maddalena-21)

After the opera's European premiere at the [Muziektheater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stopera "Stopera") in Amsterdam in June 1988, it received its first German performance later that year at the [Bielefeld Opera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bielefeld_Opera "Bielefeld Opera"), in a production by John Dew with stage designs by Gottfried Pilz.[\[22\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Neef_2000-22) In the German production, Nixon and Mao were given putty noses in what the *Los Angeles Times* considered "a garish and heavy-handed satire".[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Henken_1990-09-02-10) Also in 1988 the opera received its United Kingdom premiere, at the [Edinburgh International Festival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edinburgh_International_Festival "Edinburgh International Festival") in August.[\[23\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Scotsman_2010-03-27-23)

For the Los Angeles production in 1990, Sellars made revisions to darken the opera in the wake of [the Tiananmen Square protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tiananmen_Square_protests_of_1989 "Tiananmen Square protests of 1989"). The original production had not had an intermission between Acts 2 and 3; one was inserted, and Sellars authorized supertitles, which he had forbidden in Houston.[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Henken_1990-09-02-10) Adams conducted the original cast in the French premiere, at the Maison de la Culture de Bobigny, Paris, on December 14, 1991.[\[24\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-AmadeusOnline_Almanacco-24) Thereafter, performances of the opera became relatively rare; writing in *[The New York Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_New_York_Times "The New York Times")* in April 1996, [Alex Ross](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alex_Ross_(music_critic) "Alex Ross (music critic)") speculated on why the work had, at that time, "dropped from sight".[\[25\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Ross_1996-04-07-25)

The London premiere of the opera took place in 2000, at the [London Coliseum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London_Coliseum "London Coliseum"), with Sellars producing and [Paul Daniel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Daniel "Paul Daniel") conducting the [English National Opera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_National_Opera "English National Opera") (ENO).[\[26\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-stein-26) A revival of this production was planned for the reopening of the renovated Coliseum in 2004, but delays in the refurbishment caused the revival to be postponed until 2006.[\[27\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Jeal_2006-06-19-27) The ENO productions helped to revive interest in the work, and served as the basis of the [Metropolitan Opera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_Opera "Metropolitan Opera")'s 2011 production.[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Tommasini-28) [Peter Gelb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Gelb "Peter Gelb"), the Met's general manager, had approached Adams in 2005 about staging his operas there. Gelb intended that *Nixon in China* be the first of such productions, but Adams chose *[Doctor Atomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doctor_Atomic "Doctor Atomic")* to be the first Adams work to reach the Met.[\[29\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-gelb-29) However, Gelb maintained his interest in staging *Nixon in China*, which received its Metropolitan premiere on February 2, 2011.[\[30\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-back-30) The work received its [BBC Proms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BBC_Proms "BBC Proms") debut at the [Royal Albert Hall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Albert_Hall "Royal Albert Hall") in London on September 5, 2012, although the second-act ballet was omitted.[\[31\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Ashley_2012-09-06-31)

While a number of productions have used variations on the original staging, the February 2011 production by the [Canadian Opera Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_Opera_Company "Canadian Opera Company") used an abstract setting revived from a 2004 production by the [Opera Theatre of Saint Louis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opera_Theatre_of_Saint_Louis "Opera Theatre of Saint Louis").[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-matt-8) Alluding to Nixon's "News" aria, the omnipresence of television news was dramatized by set designer [Allen Moyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allen_Moyer "Allen Moyer") by keeping a group of televisions onstage throughout much of the action, often showing scenes from the actual visit. Instead of an airplane descending in Act 1, a number of televisions descended showing an airplane in flight.[\[32\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Everett-Green_2011-02-07-32)

Adams conducted the [Los Angeles Philharmonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_Philharmonic "Los Angeles Philharmonic") and [Los Angeles Master Chorale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_Master_Chorale "Los Angeles Master Chorale") for performances of the opera at the [Walt Disney Concert Hall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walt_Disney_Concert_Hall "Walt Disney Concert Hall") in 2017 during a series of concerts celebrating his 70th birthday. This "musically and visually dazzling reimagining of the piece" [\[33\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-33) included Super 8mm home movies of the visit to China (shot by [H. R. Haldeman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H._R._Haldeman "H. R. Haldeman"), [Dwight Chapin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwight_Chapin "Dwight Chapin"), and others) projected onto a giant screen with the appearance of a 1960s television set. In some scenes the historical footage was a backdrop that was artfully synchronized to the live cast in the foreground, in other scenes the actors were lit from behind the translucent screen appearing inside the TV, adding to the surreal experience. The props and other details were simple but effective, including the miniature souvenir program designed after Mao's [Little Red Book](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Little_Red_Book "Little Red Book").

Despite a recent proliferation of performances worldwide, the opera has not yet been shown in China.[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-matt-8)

Houston Grand Opera is again producing the opera in 2017 on the 30th anniversary of the world premiere to mixed reviews.[\[34\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-HGO_2016-01-29-34)[\[35\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-35)[\[36\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-36)

A new production was premiered at [Staatsoper Stuttgart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Staatsoper_Stuttgart "Staatsoper Stuttgart") in April 2019.

## Reception\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nixon_in_China&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Reception")\]

[![](220px-Wortham_Center.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Wortham_Center.jpg)

The [Wortham Theater Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wortham_Theater_Center "Wortham Theater Center") in Houston, Texas, venue of the world premiere, October 22, 1987

The original production in Houston received mixed reviews. *[Chicago Tribune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicago_Tribune "Chicago Tribune")* critic John von Rhein called *Nixon in China* "an operatic triumph of grave and thought-provoking beauty".[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-matt-8) *[Houston Chronicle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Houston_Chronicle "Houston Chronicle")* reviewer Ann Holmes said of the work, "The music of *Nixon* catches in your ear; I find myself singing it while whizzing along the freeways."[\[37\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Holmes_1987-11-01-37) *[Los Angeles Herald Examiner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_Herald_Examiner "Los Angeles Herald Examiner")* critic [Mark Swed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Swed "Mark Swed") wrote that it would "bear relevance for as long as mankind cherished humanity".[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-matt-8) [Martin Bernheimer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Bernheimer "Martin Bernheimer"), writing in the *[Los Angeles Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times "Los Angeles Times")*, drew attention to the choreography of Morris ("the trendy enfant terrible of modern dance") in the Act 2 ballet sequences. Morris had produced "one of those classical yet militaristic Sino-Soviet ballets from the revolutionary repertory of Mme. Mao". Bernheimer also praised "the subtle civility of Alice Goodman's couplet-dominated libretto".[\[20\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Bernheimer_1987-10-24-20)

In a more critical vein, *[The New York Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_New_York_Times "The New York Times")* chief music critic [Donal Henahan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donal_Henahan "Donal Henahan") alluded to the publicity buildup for the opera by opening his column, headed "That was it?", by calling the work "fluff" and "a Peter Sellars variety show, worth a few giggles but hardly a strong candidate for the standard repertory".[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-matt-8) *[New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_(magazine) "New York (magazine)")* magazine Peter G. Davis said that "Goodman's libretto, written in elegant couplets, reads better than it sings" and "the main trouble... is Adams's music... this is the composer's first opera and it shows, mainly in the clumsy prosody, turgid instrumentation that often obscures the words, ineffective vocal lines, and inability to seize the moment and make the stage come to life."[\[38\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-ny-38) *[St. Louis Post-Dispatch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._Louis_Post-Dispatch "St. Louis Post-Dispatch")* critic James Wierzbicki called the opera "more interesting than good ... a novelty, not much more."[\[16\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-nixonnixon-16) Television critic [Marvin Kitman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvin_Kitman "Marvin Kitman"), just prior to the telecast of the original Houston production in April 1988, stated "There are only three things wrong with *Nixon in China*. One, the libretto; two, the music; three, the direction. Outside of that, it's perfect."[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-matt-8)

The critic Theodore Bale, in his review of a revival of the opera in Houston in 2017, said he continues "to enjoy being perplexed by its deep structure and quirky contemporary aesthetic. Adam's music is constantly shimmering with some new idea, Alice Goodman's libretto is constantly surprising and eloquent, and each of the three acts offers myriad opportunities for interpretation and commentary. The opera is filled with gorgeous ensemble passages and the chorus as an entity is at the heart of the work. I have, I suppose, "used" Nixon in China for three decades as one of the finest examples of late 20th-century American opera."[\[39\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-39)

The British premiere at the 1988 Edinburgh Festival brought critical praise: "Through its sheer cleverness, wit, lyrical beauty and sense of theater, it sweeps aside most of the criticism to which it lays itself open."[\[40\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Ward_1988-09-06-40) When the work was finally performed in London, 13 years after its Houston premiere and after a long period of theatrical neglect, *Tempo'*s critic Robert Stein responded to ENO's 2000 production enthusiastically. He particularly praised the performance of Maddalena, and concluded that "Adams's triumph ... consists really in taking a plot chock-full of talk and public gesture, and through musical characterisation ... making a satisfying and engaging piece."[\[26\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-stein-26) Of the ENO revival in 2006, Erica Jeal of *[The Guardian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Guardian "The Guardian")* wrote that "from its early visual coup with the arrival of the plane, Sellars' production is an all-too-welcome reminder of his best form". In Jeal's view, the cast met admirably the challenge of presenting the work in a non-satirical spirit.[\[27\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Jeal_2006-06-19-27) Reviewing the 2008 [Portland Opera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portland_Opera "Portland Opera") production (the basis of the 2011 Canadian Opera Company presentation in Toronto), critic Patrick J. Smith concluded that "Nixon in China is a great American Opera. I suspected that it was a significant work when I saw it in 1987; I was ever more convinced of its stature when I heard it subsequently, on stage and on disc, and today I am certain that it is one of the small handful of operas that will survive."[\[41\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Smith_2005-41)

At the Met premiere in February 2011, although the audience—which included Nixon's daughter [Tricia Nixon Cox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricia_Nixon_Cox "Tricia Nixon Cox")—gave the work a warm reception,[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Tommasini-28) critical approval of the production was not uniform. Robert Hofler of *Variety* criticized Sellars for using body microphones to amplify the singing, thus compensating for the "vocally distressed" Maddalena. He further complained that the director, known for designing unorthodox settings for the operas he has staged (Hofler mentions *[The Marriage of Figaro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Marriage_of_Figaro "The Marriage of Figaro")* in the New York [Trump Tower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trump_Tower "Trump Tower") and *[Don Giovanni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Don_Giovanni "Don Giovanni")* in an urban slum), here uses visually uninteresting, overly realistic sets for the first two acts. Hofler felt that it was time that the opera received a fresh approach: "Having finally arrived at the Met, *Nixon in China* has traveled the world. It is a masterpiece, a staple of the opera repertory, and now it simply deserves a new look".[\[42\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Hofler_2011-02-03-42) However, [Anthony Tommasini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthony_Tommasini "Anthony Tommasini") of *[The New York Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_New_York_Times "The New York Times")*, while noting that Maddalena's voice was not as strong as it had been at the world premiere, maintained that due to his long association with the role, it would have been impossible to bring the opera to the Met with anyone else as Nixon: "Maddalena inhabits the character like no other singer".[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Tommasini-28) Tommasini also praised the performance of [Robert Brubaker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Brubaker_(tenor) "Robert Brubaker (tenor)") in the role of Mao, "captur\[ing\] the chairman's authoritarian defiance and rapacious self-indulgence", and found the Scottish soprano [Janis Kelly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Janis_Kelly_(soprano) "Janis Kelly (soprano)") "wonderful" as Pat Nixon.[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Tommasini-28)

Swed recalled the opera's reception in 1987 while reviewing the Metropolitan Opera's 2011 production:

> An opera that was belittled in 1987 by major New York critics – as a [CNN Opera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CNN_opera "CNN opera") of no lasting merit when Houston Grand Opera premiered it – has clearly remained relevant. Reaching the Met for the first time, it is now hailed as a classic.[\[43\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-swed-43)

## Music\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nixon_in_China&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Music")\]

*Nixon in China* contains elements of [minimalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimal_music "Minimal music"). This musical style originated in the United States in the 1960s and is characterized by stasis and repetition in place of the melodic development associated with conventional music.[\[44\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Davis_2007-44) Although Adams is associated with minimalism, the composer's biographer, [Sarah Cahill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sarah_Cahill_(pianist) "Sarah Cahill (pianist)"), asserts that of the composers classed as [minimalists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimalists "Minimalists"), Adams is "by far the most anchored in [Western classical tradition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_classical_tradition "Western classical tradition")".[\[45\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Cahill_2007-45)

Timothy Johnson contends that *Nixon in China* goes beyond minimalism in important ways. Adams had been inspired, in developing his art, by minimalist composers such as [Philip Glass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_Glass "Philip Glass"), [Steve Reich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Reich "Steve Reich"), and [Terry Riley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terry_Riley "Terry Riley"), and this is reflected in the work by repetitive rhythmic patterns. However, the opera's complex harmonic structures are very different from the simpler ones in, for example, Glass's *[Einstein on the Beach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_on_the_Beach "Einstein on the Beach")*, which Adams terms "mindlessly repetitive"; Johnson nevertheless considers the Glass opera an influence on *Nixon in China*.[\[46\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJohnson7-46) As Glass's techniques did not allow Adams to accomplish what he wanted, he employed a system of constantly shifting metric organizational schemes to supplement the repeated rhythms in the opera. The music is marked by metrical dissonance, which occurs both for musical reasons and in response to the text of the opera.[\[47\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJohnson7%E2%80%938-47)

*[The New York Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_New_York_Times "The New York Times")* critic [Allan Kozinn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allan_Kozinn "Allan Kozinn") writes that with *Nixon in China*, Adams had produced a score that is both "minimalist and eclectic ... In the orchestral interludes one hears references, both passing and lingering, to everything from [Wagner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Wagner "Richard Wagner") to [Gershwin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Gershwin "George Gershwin") and Philip Glass."[\[48\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-KozinnOMO-48) In reviewing the first recording of the work, *[Gramophone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gramophone_(magazine) "Gramophone (magazine)")'s* critic discusses the mixture of styles and concludes that "minimalist the score emphatically is not".[\[49\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Gram1-49) Other commentators have evoked "neo-classical [Stravinsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igor_Stravinsky "Igor Stravinsky")",[\[50\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Oestreich_2008-06-17-50) and concocted the term "[Mahler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_Mahler "Gustav Mahler")\-meets-minimalism", in attempts to pinpoint the opera's idiom.[\[51\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Mellor_2009-12-21-51)

The opera is scored for an orchestra without bassoons, French horns, and tuba, but augmented by saxophones, pianos, and electronic [synthesizer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthesizer "Synthesizer"). The percussion section incorporates numerous special effects, including a [wood block](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Woodblock_(instrument) "Woodblock (instrument)"), sandpaper blocks, [slapsticks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whip_(instrument) "Whip (instrument)") and [sleigh bells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jingle_bell "Jingle bell").[\[52\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Boosey-52) The work opens with an orchestral prelude of repetitive ascending phrases, after which a chorus of the Chinese military sings solemn couplets against a subdued instrumental background. This, writes Tommasini, creates "a hypnotic, quietly intense backdrop, pierced by fractured, brassy chords like some cosmic chorale", in a manner reminiscent of Philip Glass.[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Tommasini-28) Tommasini contrasts this with the arrival of Nixon and his entourage, when the orchestra erupts with "big band bursts, rockish riffs and shards of fanfares: a heavy din of momentous pomp".[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Tommasini-28) *Gramophone'*s critic compares the sharply written exchanges between Nixon, Mao and Chou En-lai with the seemingly aimless wandering of the melodic lines in the more reflective sections of the work, concluding that the music best serves the libretto in passages of rapid dialogue.[\[49\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Gram1-49) Tommasini observes that Nixon's own vocal lines reflect the real-life president's personal awkwardness and social unease.[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Tommasini-28)[\[48\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-KozinnOMO-48)

The differences in perspective between East and West are set forth early in the first act, and underscored musically: while the Chinese of the chorus see the countryside as fields ready for harvest, the fruits of their labor and full of potential, the Nixons describe what they saw from the windows of the *Spirit of '76* as a barren landscape. This gap is reflected in the music: the chorus for the workers is marked by what Johnson terms "a wide-ranging palette of harmonic colors", the Western perspective is shown by the "quick, descending, dismissive cadential gesture" which follows Nixon's description of his travels.[\[53\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJohnson22-53)

The second act opens with warm and reflective music culminating in Pat Nixon's tender aria "This is prophetic". The main focus of the act, however, is the Chinese revolutionary opera-ballet, *[The Red Detachment of Women](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Detachment_of_Women_(ballet) "Red Detachment of Women (ballet)")*, "a riot of clashing styles" according to Tommasini, reminiscent of [agitprop theatre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agitprop_theatre "Agitprop theatre") with added elements of [Strauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Strauss_II "Johann Strauss II") waltzes, blasts of jazz and 1930s Stravinsky.[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Tommasini-28)[\[49\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Gram1-49) The internal opera is followed by a monologue, "I am the wife of Mao Tse-tung" in which [Chiang Ch'ing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jiang_Qing "Jiang Qing"), Mao's wife, rails against counterrevolutionary elements in full [coloratura soprano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coloratura_soprano "Coloratura soprano") mode that culminates in a high D, appropriate for a character who in real life was a former actress given to self-dramatization.[\[a\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-55) Critic Thomas May notes that, in the third act, her "pose as a power-hungry [Queen of the Night](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Der_H%C3%B6lle_Rache_kocht_in_meinem_Herzen "Der Hölle Rache kocht in meinem Herzen") gives way to wistful regret".[\[9\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-May_2011-02-09-9) In this final, "surreal" act[\[55\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Opera_Today-56) the concluding thoughts of Chou En-lai are described by Tommasini as "deeply affecting".[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Tommasini-28) The act incorporates a brief [foxtrot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fox_Trot "Fox Trot") episode, choreographed by Morris, illustrating Pat Nixon's memories of her youth in the 1930s.[\[55\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Opera_Today-56)[\[b\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-59)

Critic Robert Stein identifies Adams's particular strengths in his orchestral writing as "motoring, brassy figures and sweetly reflective string and woodwind harmonies",[\[26\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-stein-26) a view echoed by Gregory Carpenter in the liner notes to the 2009 [Naxos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naxos_Records "Naxos Records") recording of the opera. Carpenter pinpoints Adams's "uncanny talent for recognising the dramatic possibilities of continually repeating melodies, harmonies and rhythms", and his ability to change the mix of these elements to reflect the onstage action.[\[58\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Nax-60) The feel of the Nixon era is recreated through popular music references;[\[59\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Gram2-61) Sellars has observed that some of the music associated with Nixon is derived from the big band sound of the late 1930s, when the Nixons fell in love.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-myth-3) Other commentators have noted Adams's limitations as a melodist,[\[49\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Gram1-49) and his reliance for long stretches on what critic [Donal Henahan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donal_Henahan "Donal Henahan") has described as "a prosaically chanted recitative style".[\[60\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Henahan_1987-10-24-62) However, Robert Hugill, reviewing the 2006 [English National Opera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_National_Opera "English National Opera") revival, found that the sometimes tedious "endless [arpeggios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arpeggio "Arpeggio")" are often followed by gripping music which immediately re-engages the listener's interest.[\[61\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Hugill_2006-07-02-63) This verdict contrasts with that of Davis after the original Houston performance; Davis commented that Adams's inexperience as an opera writer was evident in often "turgid instrumentation", and that at points where "the music must be the crucial and defining element ... Adams fails to do the job".[\[38\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-ny-38)

The only studio recording, made in New York by [Nonesuch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonesuch_Records "Nonesuch Records") two months after the October 1987 Houston premiere, used the same cast, just a different chorus, orchestra and conductor: [Edo de Waart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edo_de_Waart "Edo de Waart") led the Chorus and [Orchestra of St. Luke's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orchestra_of_St._Luke%27s "Orchestra of St. Luke's"). *[Gramophone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gramophone_(magazine) "Gramophone (magazine)")'s Good DVD Guide* praised the singing, noting James Maddalena's "aptly volatile Nixon" and Trudy Ellen Craney's admirable delivery of Chiang Ch'ing's coloratura passages.[\[63\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Roberts_2006-65) This recording also received the 1988 [Grammy Award](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammy_Award "Grammy Award") for Best Contemporary Composition in the Classical category.[\[64\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Grammy_NiC_88-66) It was reissued in 2011 to coincide with the opera's production at the Metropolitan Opera.[\[65\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Nonesuch_NiC-67) The Denver live recording on [Naxos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naxos_Records "Naxos Records") has [Marin Alsop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marin_Alsop "Marin Alsop") conducting the [Colorado Symphony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colorado_Symphony "Colorado Symphony") and [Opera Colorado](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opera_Colorado "Opera Colorado") Chorus, with Robert Orth as Nixon, Maria Kanyova as Pat Nixon, Thomas Hammons as Kissinger, Chen-Ye Yuan as Chou En-Lai, Marc Heller as Mao Tse-Tung and Tracy Dahl as Chiang Ch'ing.[\[58\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Nax-60) [Sumi Jo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumi_Jo "Sumi Jo") and [June Anderson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/June_Anderson "June Anderson") star as the two wives in the Paris video.

## References\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nixon_in_China&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: References")\]

1.  **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_ref-55)** "Several of the solos are direct descendants of 19th-century Italian opera archetypes: Chiang Ch'ing's bravura aria at the end of Act II ('I am the wife of Mao Tse-tung'), for example, contains coloratura runs up to a high D—a 'showpiece' aria for a character whose capacity for self-dramatization (as a former actress) was an important facet of her personality".[\[54\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-focus-54)
2.  **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_ref-59)** As "a kind of warmup for embarking on the creation of the full opera", Adams had written an extended orchestral foxtrot, *[The Chairman Dances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Chairman_Dances "The Chairman Dances")*.[\[56\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Adams_Chairman_Dances-57) Although this was not incorporated into the opera it is performed regularly as a concert piece, and has been fashioned by choreographer [Peter Martins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Martins "Peter Martins") into a ballet.[\[57\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_in_China#cite_note-Kisselgoff_1988-05-16-58)
