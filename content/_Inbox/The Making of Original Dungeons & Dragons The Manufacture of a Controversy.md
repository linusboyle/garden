_The Making of Original Dungeons & Dragons_ is curated by Jon Peterson, author of influential gaming history books like _[Playing At the World](https://refereeingandreflection.wordpress.com/2024/08/15/playing-at-the-world-round-2/)_, _[The Elusive Shift](https://refereeingandreflection.wordpress.com/2021/09/18/the-shifting-definitions-of-roleplaying/)_, and _[Game Wizards](https://refereeingandreflection.wordpress.com/2021/10/27/two-designers-enter-two-designers-leave/)_. Alongside those efforts, done independently of Wizards of the Coast, Peterson has also been brought into do the odd nice coffee table _D&D_ history book for Wizards; generally, these have been lighter, less detail-oriented, less in-depth, and more introductory in tone than his scholarly texts, as you might expect from an official history put out by a corporation aimed at a mass audience.

_The Making of OD&D_, however, is a whole other matter. As I said before, it’s massive – and most of the words in it aren’t by Peterson himself. Instead, it’s a guided tour through a range of primary documents from _D&D_ history and prehistory, spanning 1970 to 1977. Not only do you get a facsimile reproduction of the [original _Dungeons & Dragons_ boxed set](https://refereeingandreflection.wordpress.com/2016/01/11/tsr-editions-of-dd-what-are-they-good-for/) – in the version from before the cease and desist letter forced them to yank hobbits and ents and balrogs and replace them with halflings and treants and balors – but you also get full reproductions of the first three supplements, _Greyhawk_, _Blackmoor_, and _Eldritch Wizardry_.

___

_Gods, Demi-Gods & Heroes_ is not included – this included numerous figures from real-world religions and gave them _D&D_ stats, and the tone of Peterson’s discussion of it and the foreword by Jason Tondro when discussing it suggests that Wizards is embarrassed by the content and isn’t too keen to reprint it. I would also make a case that of the original run of supplements, _Gods, Demi-Gods & Heroes_ and the following _Swords & Spells_ are in no way as crucial as the first three supplements.

_Greyhawk_, _Blackmoor_, and _Eldritch Wizardry_ all introduce classes, monsters, spells, items and whatnot which got extensively grandfathered into _AD&D_ 1E and have been significant presences in all subsequent editions, so we’re looking at material whose influence reverberates through the game to this day. By contrast, _Swords & Spells_ was a mass combat system intended to be a bit more compatible with the direction _OD&D_ had developed in than _Chainmail_, the original wargame _D&D_ evolved out of, was; there’s been various attempts to tack mass combat onto _D&D_ over the years, it never sticks, _Swords & Spells_ didn’t stick.

The idea of gods and religions as a factor of play **did** stick, and was arguably intrinsic since the inclusion of the cleric in the original core rulebooks, but the specific approach taken by _Gods, Demi-Gods & Heroes_ fell by the wayside very quickly, since it largely consisted of very brief entries on each deity with associated _D&D_ stats, along with the odd monster and magic item associated with the different pantheons, without any real discussion of the pantheon as a whole or any guidance on how the material ought to be used. Confused groups generally either treated it as a high-grade monster collection or ignored it, and when _[Deities & Demigods](https://refereeingandreflection.wordpress.com/2017/08/10/theological-tomes-of-tsr-era-dd/)_ was put out for 1E it took a different approach, substantially bulking up the descriptions of the pantheons involved, being more selective about which were included, and providing more general guidance on how to use them to provide an overarching cultural perspective for clerics rather than as sources of very tough opponents. In 2E, _Legends & Lore_ moved away from attributing _AD&D_ statistics to gods directly at all, and in general subsequent editions have moved further and further away from the idea that actually giving stats to a deity makes the slightest degree of sense.

So whilst _The Making of OD&D_ doesn’t have all of the _OD&D_ supplements, it does have all the _OD&D_ supplements whose content endured in the system for the long run – and it doesn’t just have that. Interspersed in the same section as the supplement reprints are extracts from fanzines, _The Strategic Review_, and a little snippet from _Dragon Magazine_, which replaced _The Strategic Review_ towards the end of the time period discussed here, cherry-picked to either provide pointers to how the game was propagating or to showcase the origins of important game concepts, like rangers or the two-axis alignment model, both of which made their debut in magazine articles.

And when it comes to the prehistory section, there’s more besides – you get the fantasy supplement to _Chainmail_ which Dave Arneson used as the basis of his home “Braunstein”-style scenarios which formed the kernel of the Castle Blackmoor campaign that spawned _D&D_, you get correspondence between Gygax and Arneson and material from the Castles & Crusades wargame society which was making use of the _Chainmail_ rules to fight battles over the future of the Great Kingdom (establishing a background later used as the basis for the _Greyhawk_ setting), and perhaps most excitingly you have Gary Gygax’s original typewritten 1973 manuscript for _Dungeons & Dragons_, annotated by both Gygax and Arneson (putting to bed the Gygax-maximalist position that Arneson was checked out of the design process altogether), along with some interesting extracts from subsequent drafts put together by Arneson and his cronies in the Twin Cities before Gygax did the final draft and sent it to press.

If anything, reactionary gamers should be celebrating _The Making of OD&D_; if you want to harken back to the pass and think that _D&D_ was ruined once it was made woke by Wizards of the Coast/Lorraine Williams/the Hickmans/Frank Mentzer/Tom Moldvay and Zeb Cook/J. Eric Holmes and insist on playing _OD&D_ as the Great God Gygax intended, you’ve got all the tools here; this book might be a bit of a big, heavy chonker that takes up a bunch of table space, but it’s a far more imposing tome for a referee to use than the original little booklets.

And you can go even further than that – if you think _Dungeons & Dragons_ was ruined when Gygax allowed Arneson’s feedback to go against his instincts, or that Wisdom and Charisma are woke (“fellas, is it gay to be thoughtful and friendly?”) and having a stat for personal attractiveness is based (yes, Appearance was a stat in the 1973 draft, later reintroduced in _Unearthed Arcana_ as Comeliness), you can rewind the clock even further and try to run the 1973 version. Go ahead. Knock yourself out. Wizards have put all the tools in your hands and if you slap it aside because they acknowledged that Gary’s jokes about women’s lib in relation to the evil dragons being ruled by an evil queen might be a bit sexist, you’re absolutely kicking yourself in the junk to spite your face.

But there’s reasons to appreciate the book which go beyond that. Sure, _The Making of OD&D_ demonstrates just what an unwieldy mess the _OD&D_ rules are compared to later reorganisations and revisions – or, for that matter, retroclones like _Swords & Wizardry_ – but it wasn’t intended as a resource for gaming so much as it’s a treasurehouse for anyone interested in RPG history. If you’re keen on that subject and you thought _Playing At the World_ was rad, this compilation of primary sources is excellent, and really helps illustrate some of the points Peterson is making there. It’s also just a gorgeous thing to own, which is perhaps the key test of the merits of a coffee table book.
