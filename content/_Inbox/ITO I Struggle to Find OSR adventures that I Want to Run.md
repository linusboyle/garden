**Into the OSR** _is an occasional series in which I write up some of the creative decisions I have made in the preparation of my old school sandbox RPG campaign. The rest of the series can be found [here](https://tasker.land/category/series/into-the-osr/)_

![](https://tasker.land/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/marx.jpg?w=964)

I am currently six sessions deep in a hacked _Cairn 2e_ campaign set in Elizabethan East-Anglia. In an effort to crash-test both my own creativity and some theories I had about appropriate amounts of prep, I decided to write everything myself from scratch.

Then the Christmas break happened and the thought occurred to me that there might be some interesting OSR modules out there from which I could steal. The timing of this turned out to be somewhat opportune as it coincided with a particular blog post gaining a lot of traction. I must admit that said post made me rather sad as _every single one_ of the adventures recommended by that post was one of the adventures that tend to get auto-recommended whenever someone turns up at r/OSR knowing absolutely nothing about the scene.

I do not think it is _healthy_ that the same adventures are getting recommended over and over again in every situation and regardless of context. I say this as someone who plays OSR games almost exclusively while also really struggling to find any adventures that I actually want to run.

I apologise in advance for a somewhat messy post but I want to make it clear where I am coming from before I launch into the problem of social capture and why I think the same dozen-or-so adventures keep getting recommended over and over again. If you’re not interested in where I am coming from then please feel free to skip the opening section.

#### Gygaxian Materialism

Back in 2023, I wrote a post that defended ‘[Vanilla Fantasy](https://tasker.land/2023/10/24/into-the-osr-on-the-uses-of-vanilla-fantasy/)’ against the implication that bare-bones fantasy is an idiom so devoid of creative panache that it is fit only for selling loot crates to children.

While that post tacks back and forth across the surface of the topic, I think my view can be summarised as being that while aggressively weird and outlandishly creative settings may be liberating to write and pleasant to read, they are difficult to run.

The reason that ‘Vanilla Fantasy’ feels boring is that, since nerd shit came to occupy a central position in popular culture, pretty much everyone has spent a couple of hundred hours wallowing in elfbook nonsense: Maybe you have read loads of Brandon Sanderson novels, maybe you have done multiple run-throughs of _Skyrim_, maybe you have watched and re-watched all of the different adaptations and riffs on Tolkien that have appeared over the years. My point is that this stuff often feels boring because it has stopped surprising you and the reason that it has stopped surprising you is that you have consumed so much of it that you have internalised both its rules and its assumptions.

In practical terms, Vanilla Fantasy is easier to run than aggressively weird Fantasy because everyone understands how these types of setting function; minor differences of preference and interpretation might crop-up but when you say that you are running a game set in Middle-Earth then everyone is working from the same set of assumptions. Strip away those assumptions and you strip away people’s understanding of the world and if you strip away people’s understanding of the world you are impinging upon their ability to engage with it critically and thus depriving them of both creative and ludic agency.

Storygames get around this problem by explicitly normalising the idea of players being able to ‘fill in the gaps’ on their own terms but OSR games tend to rely upon the GM running or simulating the world. Presenting players with a world they do not understand is like presenting people with a hundred different unmarked leavers: Sure, they have the freedom to pull any lever they like but they are not empowered to make an informed decisions and that is a loss of agency.

All the way back in 2008, James Maliszewski wrote a famous post about what he called “[Gygaxian Naturalism](https://grognardia.blogspot.com/2008/09/gygaxian-naturalism.html)”, which is Gygax’s tendency to try and construct and populate his fictional worlds with one eye on how those worlds might operate without the presence of the player characters. So rather than just having a load of monsters waiting behind closed doors to attack the PCs, you have monsters that exist in some kind of eco-system where they have their own sets of habits and motivations:

> “A consequence of Gygaxian Naturalism is that it grounds D&D a bit more in a pseudo-reality. I don’t mean to imply that it’s realistic in any meaningful sense, only that its fantasy follows “natural” laws of a sort”

This quirk in Gygax’s style also accounts for his decidedly weird tendency to list the entire contents of a house including the mundane, near-valueless items that most GMs would be quite content to just make a ruling about (“you’re in a kitchen so… sure… you find a carving knife”).

When it comes to writing my own stuff, I don’t feel the need to painstakingly catalogue the contents of every hovel and lair, but I do feel the need to have my world make some sort of sense and spring from a set of natural laws that are either assumed or transparent enough to be discoverable through trial-and-error during play.

While this is some grade-A **nerd shit** and sits adjacent to a load of un-reconstructed discoursal moaning about ‘realism’ that is probably best consigned to the ash can of history, it is not completely idiosyncratic… as I see it, a lot of what I’m doing is contiguous with a strand of thought that runs straight through the OSR and which is very much present in documents like the [_Principia Apocrypha_](https://lithyscaphe.blogspot.com/p/principia-apocrypha.html):

![](https://tasker.land/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/pa1.jpg?w=796)

This goes almost without saying: Everything I am articulating in this post is about my preferences, my methods, and why I struggle to find adventures that I want to run. I have been playing RPGs for a long time and I know what is working for me right now, and what seems to be working for my group. I don’t know you and I don’t know what works for your group and so cannot hope to offer useful commentary. This is about _my table_.

![](https://tasker.land/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/pa2.jpg?w=798)

This principle applies to the use of random tables but the implication is that it is not only reasonable but desirable for the content of OSR games to have an underlying sense. That sense can be determined by genre expectations, tone, or any number of other things but the important thing is that verisimilitude matters and verisimilitude literally means ‘seems true’.

![](https://tasker.land/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/pa3.jpg?w=798)

Again, the _Apocrypha_ returns us to the idea that behind the contents of a campaign setting (the monsters, the maps, the NPCs) there is a logic, a common sense to which you are expected to refer when improvising or making rulings. This is where the trajectory of this post starts to arc back around to my arguments about ‘Vanilla Fantasy’ and the _shared-but-uncodified_ assumptions that underpin the fiction of an RPG campaign.

This notion is expressed even more forcefully by people operating in the even-weirder offshoot of the OSR known as Free Kriegspiel or FKR. FKR games are to NSR games as NSR games are to OSR games: Just as NSR games like _Into the Odd_ and _Cairn_ strip a load of rule mechanics out of _D&D_ and replace them with a space where stuff can be resolved through rulings and conversation, FKR strips the appartus of RPGs back even further resulting in nothing but a bit of structure,. A lot of conversation, and the occasional dice-roll.

FKR games are as light as you can get and one of their guiding principles is that [you don’t run rules… you run worlds](https://d66kobolds.blogspot.com/2020/09/free-kriegsspiel-worlds-not-rules-etc.html):

> “The point is that you enter into a world and treat it as reflexive, malleable, and responsive. You’re actors on a stage not contrived by guidelines, but by actual roads, fences, scenic byways, and other travellers. You sit down to play a game with your peers. Don’t bring your splatbooks, classes, and modifiers. Bring your imagination and the daydreams you’ve had since reading the Prose Edda”

While a life is never anything more than the accrued weight of decisions made, getting into RPGs as a teenager was one of those decisions that changed the course of my life and while there have been (extended) periods in which I not only stopped playing but also lost sight of the hobby, I usually drift back carrying with me conceptual tools acquired while off visiting other cultural climes. My last sojourn away from the hobby was spent trying to be artistic and one of the things I took from that excursion was the joy of personal expression; of taking your thoughts and ideas about the world and embedding them in the fabric of something that you create.

This where the term **Gygaxian Materialism** comes into view as while Gygax designed his adventures on the basis of what he considered to be the natural laws of his fictional universe, I design my adventures on the basis of my world-view, namely that of a cynical left-leaning anarchist and unrepentant degenerate.

From my anarchism, I take the idea that all institutions are fundamentally illegitimate in so far as they exist solely to protect and enforce arbitrary social hierarchies. From my leftism, I take Marxist historical materialism, which is the idea that the underlying logic of civilisation is that societies rely upon their modes of production. It follows from this that every institution comprising a society is shaped by its modes of production and institutions either adapt to the mode of production or face extinction. In simpler terms, every institution is a gang and every gang follows the money. One of the defining characteristics of Historical Materialism is that there is no fixed path leading from poverty and horror to prosperity and enlightenment… there’s only the deafening sound of history as competing interests grind against each other.

I (semi-jokingly) think of myself as a Gygaxian Materialist as while a Gygaxian Naturalist might look at a dungeon and say ‘There are fourteen hobgoblins but only 10 spoons so presumably no more than 10 hobgoblins can be distracted by food at any given time’, a Gygaxian Materialist might look at a campaign setting and ask themselves why the hobgoblins have not yet seized the means of production by attacking the local mill and charging exorbitant fees for the human villagers to process their grain. The Gygaxian Materialist is like Ned Beatty in _Network_:

While I will be the first to admit that I lost control of my [last OSR fantasy game](https://tasker.land/2023/05/23/into-the-osr-help-my-group-have-become-landlords/), the fact that the game descended into spreadsheets, business meetings, and player characters outsourcing the job of adventuring to less wealthy NPCs is a direct result of how I see the world and the logics that I chose to embed in my campaign setting. This brings me to the reason why I tend to struggle with a lot of OSR adventure modules…

RPGs are not a popular hobby and, within that hobby, the OSR is not a large scene. It is also a scene where there is a very pronounced [network effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Network_effect): By and large, adventure modules that are written in less popular idioms and for less popular games are less likely to be noticed than adventure modules that hue closely to the aesthetics of popular games.

People who orbit the OSR tend to speak of this as a good thing because it means that, rather than hurling words into the void, you can write for the equivalent of a market by matching your output not only to particular rule-sets but also particular vibes.

The problem is that while the network effect rewards people who go out of their way to slot their work into a particular network, the exact same effect also serves to penalise the people who wish to go in another direction, and if you are penalised for refusing to produce work that slots comfortably into a dominant aesthetic network then there are going to be less people producing out-of-network adventures.

The popularity of open licenses in the OSR means that, in principle, anyone can publish anything but in reality there are pronounced aesthetic gravity wells caused by the network effect and clustered around specific sets of social and commercial interests. Off the top of my head, the most obvious are the DCC adventures that seek to re-construct hazy teenaged D&D memories in a way that foregrounds how exciting they felt. Then there is the blended NSR whimsy of _Animal Crossing_ and the _Borrowers_ that you find orbiting _Mausritter_ or the black metal album cover aesthetics that are common in modules marketed at _Morkborg_ fans. Given that I don’t play any of those systems, I tend to filter them out and this leaves the fairy-tale dark fantasy aesthetics that are common in modules pitched with one eye on games like _Dolmenwood_ and _Cairn_.

I like and run both _Cairn_ and _OSE_ as systems but I find the fondness for fairy-tale stuff completely breaks not only my immersion but also my materialistic understanding of how the fictional worlds in these adventures are supposed to work.

On paper, I should adore Chance Dudinack’s _The Black Worm of Brandonsford_ as it is small-scale, sandboxy, and socially rooted in a village populated by interesting NPCs but then I read a section like this…

![](https://tasker.land/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/bwb.jpg?w=463)

Call me a pedant, call me closed-minded, and call me lacking in imagination but I struggle with any setting where something as LOL RANDOM as a greedy bloke turning into a dragon can happen. When I encounter that kind of thing in an adventure I feel like Ned Beatty in _Network_:

![](https://tasker.land/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/beatty.jpg?w=738)

“_You have meddled in the primeval forces of nature Mr Dudinack and I won’t have it!”_

I realise that LOL RANDOM things happening is part of the charm of fairy-tales as there’s joy to be had in the fictional transgression and perversion of natural law. Indeed, I live near a beach which is well-known for the fact that its stones have a slightly blue tint and there is a famous folktale about a blue dragon that fell asleep on the beach and slept for so long that he turned into stone, so the blue pebbles are in fact petrified dragonscale. I get it… it’s cute, it’s a nice image, and it injects a sense of mystery and wonder into an otherwise dull world.

The problem is that while I can appreciate transgression and randomness in the context of a story, I struggle with randomness when running RPGs as I spend a lot of my time as a GM thinking about how the world around my group responds to their actions: Did taking out an antagonist create a power vacuum? What would dumping thousands of gold pieces into the local economy do to prices? How might the local criminal syndicate respond to a bunch of nobodies wandering around town splashing shedloads of cash? Might the local land-owners take an interest in some weirdos who appear to be exhuming small fortunes from holes in the ground? The problem with fairy tale whimsy is that it allowed for the possibility of natural law being suspended. Tolkien’s [defence of fairy stories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On_Fairy-Stories) hinges on the idea that natural law can be suspended because gaps in the natural order allow for the possibility of divine intervention in the form of miracles (though even he allowed that suspensions of natural law need to be credible). My problem is that once you allow for the possibility that anything _can_ happen then you are draining the meaning from anything that _does_ happen.

Again, I cannot hope to speak for your preferences and what happens at your table… Most OSR people seem to absolutely adore whimsical adventures where you might encounter a talking fish or a frog who happens to be an investment banker. People love this stuff and, while I do not share those feelings, I can sort of understand where they come from. The problem is that I don’t want that type of stuff in my game and the network effect has made it really difficult for me to find published adventures that are a good fit for the kinds of games that I do want to run. This is where I loop back around to that set of recommended modules…

#### A Closed Shop

My first attempt at writing this post linked to the author of that recommendation post and while I could do the Tumblr 2004 thing of winking at him, I am in no way angry or disappointed with him. In fact, I was not familiar with the blog in question and cannot speak to the character of its owner. My issue is not so much with the post as with the fact that the post received as much traction as it did.

None of the adventures he recommends are adventures that would not be recommended in the first twelve hours of a reddit post asking for OSR recommendations. Most of these adventures were being recommended when I first started paying attention to the OSR and I suspect most of them will continue to be recommended in the future… but I don’t think that this type of recommendation list is good enough.

![](https://tasker.land/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/list.jpg?w=391)

To be blunt, this is 2025 and the OSR still has a reputation for having problems with regards to chuds and missing stairs. On this basis alone, I think that if you are going to put up a recommendation list then you need to make a bit of an effort to look beyond a bunch of white blokes. Especially when one of those white blokes has a reputation for happily accepting money from right-wing bell-ends.

Sure, it’s ‘wokeness gone mad’ and really I don’t want to be _that guy_ but complaints about the demographic makeup of recommendation lists are best understood as complaints about the fluidity of social hierarchies and while ‘sexism’, ‘racism’, and ‘homophobia’ are all relevant when explaining a lack of demographic variance, the root problem is social capture.

One of my all-time favourite scientific studies put a bunch of monkeys into cages and gave them a choice between a button that dispensed food and a button that activated footage of higher-status monkeys. The caged monkeys smashed that second button so frequently that they wound up running at a serious caloric deficit because seeing images of cool and popular people seemed more important than self-preservation.

I’m sure that I am misremembering the exact details of the study but the lesson I have always taken from it is that we are hard-wired to sort ourselves into social hierarchies and, once those pecking orders are established, we would rather _go hungry_ than admit that those pecking orders might be wrong, misguided, incomplete, or out-of-date.

OSR recommendation lists tend to feature not only the same adventures but also the same names over and over again… If a particular adventure on such a list is new then chances are that the name attached to it is not and most of the adventures are familiar and prone to drawing on the same narrow range of dominant aesthetics: Everyone knows _Tomb of the Serpent Kings_ is a great introduction to old school play, everyone knows that _Hole in the Oak_ is a celebrated larger dungeon that supports quite a few sessions of play, and everyone knows that it is a good aesthetic and tonal fit for individual modules such as _Winter’s Daughter,_ _Black Worm of Brandonsford_, and _The Waking of Willowby Hall_. Every single one of these adventures would be recommended almost instantly if you were to start a recommendation thread on r/OSR.

The problem is not a lack of options… Over New Year there were no less than five separate posts calling for people to start publishing their own adventures and the only thing I could think about is that DriveThruRPG awards a copper badge for [51-units sold](https://help.drivethrupartners.com/hc/en-us/articles/12780761951255-Bestseller-Metals-and-How-to-Earn-Badges). That is not a lot of units to shift… The money generated by 51 sales would probably struggle to cover the costs of Affinity publisher and the various other apps required to produce an OSR module and that is before you even start to consider the possibility of paying for art. 51 sales is _almost nothing_ and yet consider how few modules on DriveThruRPG currently have a copper badge… we are _drowning_ in OSR modules and yet everyone keeps recommending the same narrow list of titles over and over again.

This is a problem that I feel quite acutely as while I play OSR games almost exclusively, I have not brought an OSR fantasy module to the table in _years_. I’ve run TSR stuff and I’ve run quite a few _Liminal Horror_ adventures but when I try to find an OSR fantasy module that fits my sensibility let alone the details of an existing campaign, I don’t even know where to start looking. That is a discoverability problem, which is another reason why I have some sympathy for the person who posted the recommendation list: He’s literally just repeating what he’s been told.

#### The Need for Social Complexity

One of my all-time favourite books is _The Collapse of Complex Societies_ by Joseph A. Tainter. Tainter is an anthropologist and he describes the evolution of different civilisations in terms of ‘energy’ and ‘social complexity’.

The idea is that, as societies expand, they accumulate ‘energy’ in terms of people power and raw materials. At first, societies expand by finding ways to acquire additional sources of energy but there comes a point at which the costs of acquiring these new energy sources outweighs the benefits of having them as flat-lining productivity combined with increasing demand will eventually result in a net loss.

Productive societies solve this bottleneck by acquiring ‘social complexity’ in the sense that they will create specific social and economic roles that serve to improve productivity and increase the yield of existing energy sources resulting in said society generating more energy despite not acquiring any fresh sources of energy.

To consider the OSR in terms of Tainter, the call for yet-more-people to produce yet-more-dungeons is a call for the generation of yet-more-energy. The people who will be working on the production of their first module tomorrow are the people who are today setting up sewing circles or running the Discord server for a medium-sized swinging clade. Calls for more modules are calls for the energy that might otherwise go into organising a ‘Tarts and Vicars’ party to be redirected into producing low level _OSE_ modules with names like _The Awkwardness at Ploppington Falls_.

The problem is not that the OSR is lacking in energy… there’s loads of energy in the scene… people are producing shit constantly… the problem is that a lot of the energy that is currently being generated is being _wasted_ because we have a social capture problem and people are busy posting lists that amplify existing social hierarchies without really bothering to look beyond a group of people whose names are already on the map. A lot of the energy that is currently going into the OSR is going into producing (potentially great) adventures that don’t get noticed, don’t get purchased, don’t get played, don’t get reviewed, don’t get discussed, and wind up having zero influence. Anyone can put time, money, and energy into a socially ossified scene but they will only ever do so _once_ and nobody’s first adventure is ever their best.

To be perfectly frank, I am mindful of the people whose work doesn’t make it onto broadly-circulated recommendation lists because I am not being catered to by the most popular forms of OSR adventure design and while it’s a distinct possibility that literally nobody is writing gloomily left-wing fantasy sandboxes, I suspect that I am just failing to see them because nobody is discussing them. In fact, I only became aware of my all-time favourite OSR sandbox adventure [_Bad Kansas City_](https://tasker.land/2024/05/28/review-bad-kansas-city-haunt-of-the-pendergeist/) because it was suggested to me by the recommendation algorithms on Amazon!

Tainter would suggest that while the OSR scene needs social complexity much more than it needs additional energy as clearly there is a real need for people who are willing to sort through all of the stuff that is currently appearing on Itch and DriveThruRPG only to be roundly ignored in favour of the same narrow range of adventures that always wind up being recommended.

Building a critical hinterland is hard and the hobby has never really had one before; Rather than cultivating a class of discoverers and appreciators, the hobby has historically favoured remaining steadfastly and uncritically loyal to companies whose editorial standards have historically struggled to rise above the level of ‘is this manuscript covered in marinara sauce?’

I am also aware of the difficulty of building a critical scene from my own reluctance to do any of the work myself. This blog exists to serve my game and not the other way around… I have no desire to start dragging friends and family through random dungeons and _Call of Cthulhu_ adventures purely for the sake of generating content and yet, I am genuinely moved by the people who remain unseen.

Despite having [a blanket policy of refusing any and all review copies](https://tasker.land/2023/09/05/the-gardens-at-taskerland-v3-1-reviews-policy/), 2024 saw me receive over 100 queries and unsolicited submissions of stuff to review. While some of these were from people who were clearly too pushy, lazy, or addled to read my reviews policy, a lot of these queries were from people who were just desperate to be seen… people who had burned the midnight oil designing, writing, and re-writing some weird document only for it to be met with overwhelming and deafening silence.

As someone who has been creative in the past, I have shown my work to friends and family and found their reactions entirely unsatisfying because my pre-existing relationships with them predisposed them towards both kindness and charity but these are not the things I need in order to grow. I can completely understand the need to have some stranger look at your work and respond to it… even if it is just to tell you that it’s a load of impenetrable old shit and that you should go back to watching porn instead of trying to write adventures.

I have enormous amounts of sympathy for these people and I think the lack of reviewers in the RPG scene is a terrible, _terrible_ shame but I also know that my tastes are evidently so far outside the mainstream of the hobby that I have spent six months trying to find a single adventure that was a good fit for what I want to do at my table. I suspect that there are adventures out there for me and I read review blogs and listen to review podcasts whenever I can but I am still at a loss as to where to start digging and that is incredibly frustrating for me as a consumer and as someone who enjoys writing reviews.
